1. Introduction

This documentation is a follow-up to the Ozone Prototyping Summary, which details the parameters and motivations for pursuing an effective ozone disinfection system for the Airbitat portable. This document is written with the assumption that the previous document has been read, as various references and concepts here will reference the aforementioned document.

An alternative chemical based treatment is pursued in order to better tackle the following issues:

  • The growth of bioactivity in the water tank
  • The growth of biofilm in the water tank
  • The development of a fishy smell in the evaporative pads
  • The subsequent need to perform regular, on site maintenance of Airibtat units

3. Chlorine Treatment

Chlorine is a highly disinfective chemical, akin to ozone in its highly oxidising nature. Chlorine normally dissociates in water to form hypochlorite ions via an equilibrium reaction with water. This hypochlorite ion provides disinfective properties in the aqeuous form, reacting with and killing bacteria. In this process, the pH of the water also rises.

There are multiple ways of introducing chlorine into a system - as a gas directly, liquid or solid. A summarised and thorough overview of various chlorination methods can be found here. A table of the various solid-based chlorination methods are as such:


Fig 3.1 - Summary of Chlorine Treatment in Water (Source)


While gaseous and liquid chlorination is possible, solid-based chlorination was explored further, owing to the convenience and ease of administering the disinfection treatment. Two of the solid-based treatments were then explored, namely hypochlorite (hypo) and trichloroisocyanurate (or trichlor).

Unlike the previous analysis with ozone involving long term continuous testing, all chlorine treatments are done once at the start of the week, and ORP as well as free chlorine is evaluated during the one hour period after chlorine is added. This is known as a shock treatment as chlorine is only added once every week, and not administered every hour like ozone (which is conversely known as a continuous treatment).

3.1 - Measuring Chlorine

Chlorine can be measured directly or indirectly. This section will better explain how these two methods when used together can complement one another in determining disinfection efficacy.

3.1.1 - Direct Chlorine Measurement

Chlorine in water is normally defined as:

Total Chlorine (TC) = Combined Chlorine (CC) + Free Chlorine (FC)

The three different forms of chlorine are better explained:

  1. Total Chlorine - This is the total amount of chlorine of all forms that is present in the water, basically the total initial amount of chlorine that we added
  2. Combined Chlorine - This is the amount of chlorine that has reacted with contaminants or bacteria in the water, and has been consumed
  3. Free Chlorine - This is whatever chlorine of all forms that is leftover, or the remaining amount of chlorine in the water after disinfection is complete

With this split, the effectiveness of chlorine treatment is usually measured by how much free chlorine there is in the water, as a higher amount of free chlorine would mean that the chlorine has a higher capacity to continue to disinfect whatever remaining bacteria there is in the water. Free chlorine can be measured in the laboratory via a colorimeter: free chlorine in the water turns bright pink in the presence of DPD, and the colour intensity of this reaction is measured to back calculate the concentration of free chlorine in water with Beer-Lambert’s Law.

In general, water is considered fully disinfected at concentrations of about >1.5mg/L of free chlorine, with cooling towers normally operating at a baseline of 0.5mg/L of free chlorine throughout its life cycle to ensure long-term disinfection.

Aside from achieving disinfection goals, discharge limits are particularly important to take note of for chlorine-based treatments. The National Environmental Agency has a full list of watercourse discharge limits found here. Crucially, chlorine-based items have the following discharge limits:

Chemical Concentration Limits (mg/L)
Free Chlorine 1.0
Chloride Ion 250
pH 6-9
Trihalomethanes (THMs) 0.08
Haloacetic Acids (HAAs) 0.06
Fig 3.2 - Discharge limits of chlorine-influenced products


THMs and HAAs are biproducts formed from the reaction of chlorine in the water with organic substances (which can come from bacteria). While they are normally almost 0, it is important to check the water discharge concentrations of all the above listed key substances when engaging in chlorine based treatments.

3.1.2 - Indirect Chlorine Measurement

Chlorine can also be measured via oxidation-reduction potential, or ORP like that in ozone. A useful scale of 2-log removal disinfection with ORP is as follows:


Fig 3.3 - Disinfection Efficacy with ORP scale for various bacteria (Source)


Unlike ozone, chlorine concentration in water does not follow a fixed correlation with ORP, as ORP changes with chlorine concentration as well as pH of the water, hence it is normally very difficult to directly deduce the amount of chlorine in the water from the ORP alone. An in-depth, thorough explanation of this phenomenon can be found here.

Nevertheless, with Fig 3.3 above it is useful to keep in mind that for disinfection purposes, the ORP of the water should be kept at anywhere above 600 mV for about 5 minutes to ensure 2-log removal of most bacteria. Again, ORP is directly measured in water with the ORP meter used also in the ozone treatment.

With comparisons done between the indirect and direct measures of chlorine in water, alongside the results of water-tests for various ions, the three chlorine based treatments are evaluated against one another in the following sections.

3.2 - Free Chlorine Comparison

In order to determine the required dose to add into the system, a simple estimate of the desired total chlorine was decided. To calculate the amount of total chlorine in the system, an equation like such is used, which explanations below:

\[Cl_2 \,(mg/L) \rightarrow Mass \,of\, Product \times Available\, Chlorine (\%) \div 65\, litres\]

  1. Mass of Product: Mass of raw solid chlorine-based product added
  2. Available Chlorine: Determined from Figure 3.1 above, which describes the amount by weight of total chlorine available from the original chlorine product’s mass
  3. 65 litres: The average amount of water in the Airbitat water tank at any point of time

Hence for an available chlorine of 65% and 90% for hypochlorite and trichloroisocyanurate respectively, a first basis of comparison was done between 1.5g dose of hypochlorite and 1.2g of trichlor to theoretically give the same amount of total chlorine in the water. For both initial tests, the HRU/MCU fans were turned off as it was theorised that the fans would reduce the amount of free chlorine in the water given the increased rate of chlorine gas lost into the surrounding air with the fans on.

Thereafter, different trichlor masses of 0.9g (shown in Appendix) and 1.5g were also explored to offer more points of comparison, as well as the turning on of the fans to test the aforementioned theory. The results of the tests as seen below:


Fig 3.4 - Free Chlorine Comparison


The following conclusions can be drawn:

  • In comparing the 1.2g Trichlor w/o fans with the 1.5g Hypo w/o fans, there a slightly higher free chlorine concentration initially for trichlor peaking at around 2.6 mg/L, before falling below hypo at the 20 minute mark.
    • From comparing the area under both curves, there seems to be rather equal total amount of free chlorine deposited in the water after the entire 60 minutes.
    • However, hypochlorite seems to have less free chlorine in the water at the end of 60 minutes as compared to trichlor
  • In comparing 1.2g Trichlor w/o fans and 1.2g Trichlor with fans, there is a higher amount of free chlorine when the fans are off throughout the entire period, suggesting turning on the MCU/HRU fans cause free chlorine to escape in the surrounding air.
  • Lastly, the increase in dosing from 1.2g Trichlor with fans to the 1.5g Trichlor with fans drastically increases the amount of free chlorine in the water, suggesting that the amount of chlorine needed in the water to react with bacteria has already been met with the 1.2g dose (hence any extra chlorine added just becomes free chlorine).

A mental note should be given to the discharge limit of free chlorine at 1.0 mg/L, and it is important to identify as such that each of the set ups have various times taken before the water is safe to discharge. Hence, an advisory of about >90 minutes before discharge is highly recommended for all set ups.

3.3 - ORP Comparison

To accompany the free chlorine results, ORP was also logged down:

Fig 3.5 - ORP Comparison


From the ORP results, we can also confirm the following things:

  • In comparing the 1.2g Trichlor w/o fans with the 1.5g Hypo w/o fans, there is virtually the same rate of increase and decrease of ORP through the first 40 minutes (given the same mass of total chlorine in the water), however hypochlorite seems to fall quicker after the 40 minute mark, which also corresponds to the free chlorine pattern observed earlier.
  • In comparing 1.2g Trichlor w/o fans and 1.2g Trichlor with fans, the ORP is much higher for the latter which would seem contradictory to the trend observed in free chlorine noted earlier. This might be due to the higher initial ORP of the 1.2g Trichlor with fans at about 330 mV as compared to 1.2g Trichlor w/o fans at 270 mV, showing susceptibility of ORP to starting conditions (other substances in the water causing ORP to be higher than what is expected)
  • Lastly, when comparing 1.2g Trichlor with fans to the 1.5g Trichlor with fans, the same anamoly is observed as noted as the latter has a lower ORP value throughout the 80 minutes despite the higher dosing.

Through both evaluations, the minimum disinfection targets are achieved in all set ups (>1.5 mg/L free chlorine and 600 mV),

Although hypochlorite and trichlor were noted to be largely equal in efficacy from these tests, trichlor is able to achieve the same disinfection with a smaller mass of 1.2g as compared to 1.5g in hypochlorite given the higher available chlorine. As such, it was concluded that long term chemical dosing would likely involve exclusively trichlor, and additional secondary tests such as water and smell tests were also conducted to further ascertain trichlor’s effectiveness, which will be found in Section 5.

4. Zinc Anodes

Zinc is commonly used as a sacrificial metal in other industrial applications, as it has a fairly negative electrode potential, hence allowing it to oxidise into zinc ions in place of other more electropositive/reactive metals like iron and copper when placed in water together.

The application of zinc for bioactivity control is not well understood in current literature, with few papers like one found here that describe the potential for zinc as an antimicrobial agent. Nonetheless, because zinc is an essential metal ion in all living cells, an excess of it (as with any other essential metal ion like Hg, Cu, Pb etc.) is understood to be toxic as it inhibits normal cell respiratory function.

Multiple companies like Anode Systems have developed easy to use and apply zinc anodes to place in water that have a strong ability to remove odour in water. In the application of the Airbitat system, a zinc “sunflower” as marketed by Anode Systems was attached to a more electropositive metal (copper or iron rod) and placed in the water tank, allowing the zinc metal to slowly release zinc ions in the water over time.

The zinc sunflower quickly loses its shiny surface as the metal begins to undergo pitting corrosion. The pitting process is accelerated when:

  1. The surface area of the metal exposed to water is higher
  2. Electronegativity difference between the metals connected (difference in Zn to Fe < difference in Zn to Cu) to one another is greater

Below are two comparison photos of the zinc sunflower attached to an iron rod, with a 4 week difference between them:

Nonetheless, alongside the chlorine treatment, zinc anodes were further evaluated in efficacy by conducting water tests and smell tests, which will be elaborated in the next section.

5. Preliminary Results

To conduct long term testing of the units, three units were utilised to run variations of the two parameters to test efficacy in removing bioactivity and smell:

  1. Unit 85 - Zinc Stick for two weeks (7th - 19th Nov), Zinc Sunflower running 3 weeks as of 11th December
  2. Unit 88 - Zinc Sunflower (30th Oct onwards), running 6 weeks as of 11th December
  3. Unit SS - Trichlor only, ran 0.9g and thereafter 1.5g doses weekly

As such currently as of 11th Dec, there are two zinc sunflower units and one plain trichloroisocynurate unit.

5.2 - Smell Tests

A quick recap of how to check for the smell of the evaporative pads are as follows:

  1. The pump has to be turned off, so that there is no fresh water running through the evaporative pads to mask the smell
  2. The fans have to be turned on so that the pads are drying out
  3. The smell will be strongest when the pads are in a semi-dry state, which usually takes around 20-30 minutes after the pumps are turned off

Although the trichlor shock treatment seems to quantitatively solve the bioactivity issues in the water tank, it is an ineffective long-lasting solution. Conversely, the zinc unit seems to be effective in suppressing the the foul smell coming from the evaporative pads for up to 3 to 4 weeks.

  1. While it was previously assumed that smell of the evaporative pads would be correlated with the amount of bioactivity in the water (SPC), it was discovered through the water tests with Zinc that the amount of SPC is not strongly correlated with the smell of the unit
  2. Instead the amount of SRBs is better correlated with the smell of the unit

However, with the lack of long term results, this conclusion is a weak one and more observations are required to draw a fair conclusion.

6.2 - Other pending observations

As this test is still ongoing, some avenues to continue to explore can be broadly classified under chlorine, zinc and other chemical questions.

6.2.1 - Pending chlorine questions

Chlorine is a very oxidising, damaging chemical which dissolves in water to form small amounts of hydrochloric acid, which when examined in the long term, causes many metals to oxidise rapidly if not added in controlled doses. As such, some of the following variables will need to be analysed:

  1. Metal oxidation - The iron (pump) and copper (heat exchanger) based parts in the Airbitat will need to be examined for long term damage, as chlorine should over time corrode and cause the metal to pit. For example, a paper examining chlorine damage on steel can be found here.

  2. Non-metal corrosion - Other non-metal parts like rubber and PVC should be normally resistant to chlorine corrosion, however some long term damage or deposition might be observed nonetheless and should be visually inspected.

  3. Efficacy in preventing biofilm growth - While proven effective in lowering the amount of bioactivity, chlorine has not been fully measured in preventing biofilm growth from a clean unit (though based on pictures, the weekly chlorine-dosed unit (Social Space) seems to take longer to develop biofilm than the zinc units (85 and 88))

6.2.2 - Pending zinc questions

Because zinc is still not well understood in its function, there are more miscellaneous questions to address with the use of zinc:

  1. Compatibility with other metals - As zinc ions are discharged into the water, it should naturally deposit onto more electropositive metals, hence the iron (pump) and copper (heat exchanger) based parts need to be examined for zinc deposition, though at the 6 week mark there is no evidence of zinc deposition anywehre.

  2. Zinc Resistance - Zinc ion efficacy in disinfection will diminish over time (as with any other essential metal ion) as the bacteria begins to develop a resistance mechanism to the metal ion. Resistance mechanisms are never fully understood, but papers such as that found here offer a bit of insight to zinc resistence specifically. With regards to the Airbitat, an exact period of time it takes for water-borne bacteria to develop zinc resistance needs to be quantified.

  3. Zinc lifespan - A followup to the previous point, besides ascertaining how long the bacteria takes to develop a sizeable resistance to zinc, we also need to determine how long a zinc anode can last in the water tank before it needs to be replaced (1 year according to sunflower manufacturer)

  4. Efficacy in preventing biofilm growth - Same as the chlorine above, zinc does not seem very effective in preventing biofilm growth.

6.2.3 - Pending misc. questions

There are additional, untested chemicals that have yet to be tested and can be explored should the zinc option become especially expensive:

  1. Copper/Silver Anode - As useful starting point to investigate would be to explore pool operators that use this technology, such as that found here. While it was initially concluded to be infeasible due to cost, it is included here for documentation.

  2. Chlorine/Zinc Suppliers - While granular trichlor and the zinc sunflower are being used at the moment, there are alternatives to both suppliers available online. An alternative for example for the granular trichlor would be to custom-make 1.5g trichlor tablets, and for zinc it would be to use other sacrificial zinc anodes. However, both methods while feasibile in theory must be re-evaluated in efficacy with the methods above to ensure replicability of the disinfection results.

  3. Zinc and Chlorine compatibility - A granular trichlor dose of 1.5g was done for the weekly dosed ordinary unit (SS), and for the zinc unit which had been running for 6 weeks without dosing (88), and the free chlorine results are depicted below:


Fig 6.2 - Free Chlorine Comparison with and without zinc


The maximum free chlorine observerable by the free chlorine meter is 2.5 ppm, hence the results for chlorine above 2.5ppm are estimated. Nevertheless, there seems to be an obvious conclusion to draw:

  • There is less free chlorine available in Unit 88 (With Zinc Sunflower) as compared to Social Space (Without Zinc), suggesting that the chlorine demand of the former unit is higher, resulting in chlorine being consumed.

There are likely two sources of chlorine demand in the zinc sunflower unit that are different from the weekly-dosed social space unit:

  • Presence of Zinc Metal - Chlorine can react with the aqeuous zinc ions to form soluble zinc chloride. Zinc chloride is thankfully not hazardous, but should nevertheless be further investigated into to determine if it can severely reduce the amount of chlorine available for disinfection.

  • More bioactivity in unit 88 - As the zinc unit had not been dosed in 6 weeks as compared to the social space unit that is dosed every week, more chlorine might have been consumed to kill increased amount of bacteria and bioactivity. This needs to be cross-verified with the water test results (which will be between SSG5 (before dose),SSG6 (after dose) and 88I6 (before dose), 88I7 (after dose)).

Additional compability tests between chlorine and zinc might need to be tested if the amount of free chlorine in the unit becomes too low over time.

7. Appendix

7.1 - Other Parameters Tested

In this section, three alternative (unsuccessful) chemical tests are detailed and obervations are recorded:

7.1.1 - Dichloroisocyanurate

A solid-based chlorine product, dichlor is principally similar to both hypochlorite and trichloroisocyanurate, releasing total chlorine into the water to perform disinfection. Commercially it is readily available at pharmacies and is known as troclosene sodium, or Aquatabs, and is designed to be added to raw water in order to make potable water. Given the small volume required in drinking water, multiple tablets adding up to 1.4g of active agent dichlor (equivalent to 1.2g trichlor) were added to water to test the amount of free chlorine produced as follows:


Fig 7.1 - Dichloroisocyanurate Pills


As seen, each individual 1.5g pill only contained about 8.5mg of dichlor and hence multiple pills needed to be administered to add up the active agent. In the end, although the amount of free chlorine tested in water was about the same as an equal mass of trichlor, the simplicity of using trichlor was opted for. This product was never tested in the Airbitat unit.

7.1.2 - Calcium Sulfate-Dihydrate Silica

This block is normally marketed as anti-smell block, and should in theory bind with foul smelling hydrogen sulfide to remove the odour from the evaporative media pads.


Fig 7.2 - Calcium Sulfate-Dihydrate Silica Block


This block was tested from the period of 21st October - 27th October 2018. However after 1 week of leaving it in the Airbitat, the foul smell still developed as per normal, and hence this chemical was deemed to have failed.

7.1.3 - Tea Tree Oil

Finally, this product was claimed to also similarly remove foul smells by masking it with a tea tree odour.


Fig 7.3 - Tea Tree Oil


The tea tree oil was tested on the 19th November 2018. While the tea tree odour was certainly evident for the period immediately after being administered, it was also unable to remove any foul smell and hence deemed to have failed.
